# 1. Introduction

Entropy is one of the most important concepts in thermodynamics.

Many students think entropy simply means **disorder**.

Although this description is useful initially, it is **not the complete definition**.

A better thermodynamic interpretation is:

> **Entropy is a measure of the energy that is no longer available to perform useful work.**

During every natural process, some useful work is lost.

This lost work appears as an increase in entropy.

---

# 2. Why Do We Need Entropy?

The **First Law of Thermodynamics**

```text
dU = δQ − δW
```

tells us that **energy is conserved**.

However, it **cannot answer** questions such as

- Why does heat always flow from hot to cold?
- Why do gases mix automatically?
- Why can't spontaneous processes reverse themselves?
- Why is some work permanently lost?

To answer these questions, we introduce a new thermodynamic property:

> **Entropy (S)**

---

# 3. Thermodynamic Equilibrium

A system is in thermodynamic equilibrium when

- No spontaneous change occurs.
- Temperature is uniform.
- Pressure is uniform.
- Composition is uniform.
- The system remains in a state of rest.

For an ideal gas,

```text
PV = RT
```

If Pressure and Temperature are fixed,

Volume is automatically fixed.

The system is therefore completely defined.

---

# 4. Spontaneous Process

A spontaneous (natural) process occurs without any external assistance.

The system naturally moves

```text
Non-equilibrium
        │
        ▼
   Equilibrium
```

Characteristics

- Natural
- Irreversible
- Directional
- Ends at equilibrium

---

# 5. Reversible and Irreversible Processes

## Reversible Process

A reversible process proceeds through a series of equilibrium states.

It is infinitely slow.

Maximum work is obtained.

Examples

- Reversible isothermal expansion
- Reversible compression

---

## Irreversible Process

An irreversible process occurs naturally.

The system passes through non-equilibrium states.

Some useful work is always lost.

Examples

- Heat transfer
- Mixing of gases
- Free expansion
- Friction

---

# 6. Examples of Spontaneous Processes

## Heat Transfer

If

```text
T₁ > T₂
```

then

```text
Heat : T₁ → T₂
```

Heat never flows spontaneously from cold to hot.

---

## Mixing of Gases

Initially

```text
Gas A | Gas B
```

After removing the partition

```text
Gas A + Gas B
```

Observations

- Mixing is spontaneous.
- Mixing is random.
- Separation does not occur naturally.

---

# 7. Lost Work

Consider an isolated system.

```text
δQ = 0
```

Therefore,

```text
dU = −δW
```

Initially,

the system has energy capable of performing useful work.

As the spontaneous process proceeds,

the amount of useful work continuously decreases.

At equilibrium,

```text
δW = 0
```

No more useful work can be extracted.

This unavailable work is called

> **Lost Work**

---

# 8. Relationship Between Lost Work and Entropy

This is the most important idea in thermodynamics.

```text
Useful Work
      │
      ▼
 Lost Work
      │
      ▼
 Increase in Entropy
```

Therefore,

> **Entropy is the thermodynamic measure of lost work.**

---

# 9. Free Expansion of an Ideal Gas

## Experimental Setup

- One chamber contains an ideal gas.
- The second chamber is evacuated.
- The partition is removed.
- The system is isolated.

The gas expands freely into vacuum.

---

### Applying the First Law

Since the system is isolated,

```text
δQ = 0
```

Expansion occurs against vacuum.

Therefore,

```text
Pext = 0
```

Hence,

```text
δW = Pext dV = 0
```

Therefore,

```text
dU = 0
```

Since internal energy of an ideal gas depends only on temperature,

```text
ΔT = 0
```

---

## Conclusion

Free expansion is

- Isothermal
- Irreversible
- Spontaneous
- No heat transfer
- No work done

---

# 10. Reversible Expansion

Suppose the same expansion occurs reversibly.

Maximum work obtained is

```text
Wrev = RT ln(V₂/V₁)
```

For free expansion,

```text
W = 0
```

Hence,

```text
Wlost = Wrev
```

All reversible work is lost.

---

# 11. Definition of Entropy

Entropy is defined using a reversible process.

```text
dS = δQrev / T
```

For a finite process,

```text
ΔS = Qrev / T
```

Important

Even if the actual process is irreversible,

entropy is always calculated using an equivalent reversible path.

---

# 12. Entropy Change During Free Expansion

Since

```text
Qrev = RT ln[(V₁ + V₂)/V₁]
```

Therefore,

```text
ΔSsystem = R ln[(V₁ + V₂)/V₁]
```

Although

```text
Q = 0
W = 0
```

entropy still increases.

---

# 13. Entropy of the Universe

```text
ΔSuniverse = ΔSsystem + ΔSsurroundings
```

For an isolated system,

```text
ΔSsurroundings = 0
```

Therefore,

```text
ΔSuniverse > 0
```

---

# 14. Second Law of Thermodynamics

The Second Law states

> The entropy of an isolated system never decreases.

Mathematically,

For spontaneous processes

```text
ΔSuniverse > 0
```

For reversible processes

```text
ΔSuniverse = 0
```

For impossible processes

```text
ΔSuniverse < 0
```

---

# 📋 Formula Sheet

```text
PV = RT

dU = δQ − δW

δQ = δW      (Isothermal)

δQ = 0       (Adiabatic)

dU = −δW

Wrev = RT ln(V₂/V₁)

dS = δQrev / T

ΔS = Qrev / T

ΔSsystem = R ln[(V₁+V₂)/V₁]

ΔSuniverse = ΔSsystem + ΔSsurroundings

ΔSuniverse > 0
```

# 💡 Key Concepts

✔ Entropy is **not energy**.

✔ Entropy is a **state function**.

✔ Entropy depends only on the initial and final states.

✔ Every spontaneous process increases entropy.

✔ Every spontaneous process is irreversible.

✔ Entropy measures the loss of useful work.

✔ At equilibrium, entropy is maximum for an isolated system.

---

# ⚠️ Common Misconceptions

❌ Entropy always means disorder.

✔ Disorder is only an interpretation.

The thermodynamic definition is

> Entropy measures the unavailable energy (lost work).

---

❌ Heat transfer always changes temperature.

✔ During free expansion,

```text
Q = 0
ΔT = 0
```

yet entropy increases.

---

❌ Energy is destroyed.

✔ Energy is conserved.

Only the **ability to perform useful work decreases.**

# 📝 Frequently Asked Exam Questions

### Q1. What is entropy?

Entropy is a thermodynamic property that measures the unavailable energy (lost work) of a system.

---

### Q2. Why does entropy increase during spontaneous processes?

Because a part of the useful work becomes unavailable during every spontaneous process.

---

### Q3. Why is entropy calculated using reversible heat?

Because entropy is a state function, and the reversible path provides a unique way to evaluate the entropy change between two equilibrium states.

---

### Q4. Can entropy decrease?

- **System:** Yes, under suitable conditions.
- **Universe:** No, for a spontaneous process.

---

## ⭐ Final Conclusion

> **Entropy is the thermodynamic property that determines the direction of natural processes.**

It tells us how much energy has become unavailable for useful work and why all spontaneous processes proceed toward equilibrium.
