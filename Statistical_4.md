# 1. Introduction

In the previous sections, we learned

- A macrostate consists of many microstates.
- Different macrostates have different multiplicities (Ω).
- Entropy is given by

```text
S = k ln Ω
```

Now we ask

> **Which macrostate will the system naturally choose?**

The answer is

> **The macrostate having the largest multiplicity (Ω).**

This state is called the **equilibrium state**.

---

# 2. What is Thermodynamic Equilibrium?

A system is said to be in **thermodynamic equilibrium** when

- No spontaneous change occurs.
- All macroscopic properties remain constant.
- Temperature is uniform.
- Pressure is uniform.
- Chemical composition is uniform.

Once equilibrium is reached,

the system has **no tendency to change further**.

---

# 3. Probability of a Macrostate

Suppose

there are

```text
16 possible microstates.
```

These microstates are grouped into five macrostates.

| Macrostate | Number of Microstates (Ω) | Probability |
|------------|--------------------------:|------------:|
| I | 1 | 1/16 |
| II | 4 | 4/16 |
| III | 6 | 6/16 |
| IV | 4 | 4/16 |
| V | 1 | 1/16 |

Notice

Macrostate III has

```text
Ω = 6
```

which is the largest value.

Therefore,

it is also the most probable macrostate.

---

# 4. Why is One Macrostate More Probable?

Imagine throwing

4 identical coins.

There are

```text
16 possible outcomes.
```

Outcome

```text
HHHH
```

occurs only

```text
1 way.
```

Outcome

```text
HHTT
```

occurs

```text
6 different ways.
```

Therefore,

you observe

```text
HHTT
```

far more frequently than

```text
HHHH.
```

Exactly the same idea applies to molecules.

Nature simply prefers

the state

having the largest number of possible arrangements.


# 6. Maximum Multiplicity

Nature always evolves toward

```text
Maximum Ω
```

because

there are simply

more microscopic arrangements available.

This is a purely statistical argument.

The system does not "choose" the state consciously.

Instead,

there are overwhelmingly more ways for the system to exist in that state.

---

# 7. Maximum Entropy

Boltzmann's Equation

```text
S = k ln Ω
```

shows that

entropy increases whenever

Ω increases.

Therefore,

```text
Maximum Ω

↓

Maximum Entropy
```

Since

nature always evolves toward

maximum Ω,

it also evolves toward

maximum entropy.

---

# 8. Statistical Interpretation of the Second Law

The classical Second Law states

```text
ΔSuniverse > 0
```

Statistical Thermodynamics explains

**why**.

A spontaneous process

```text
↓

Ω increases

↓

Entropy increases

↓

System approaches equilibrium
```

Therefore,

the Second Law is simply

a statistical consequence of

the enormous number of microscopic arrangements.

---

# 9. Why Doesn't Entropy Decrease Naturally?

In principle,

a system **can**

move toward

a smaller Ω.

However,

the probability is

extremely small.

Consider

```text
Ω = 10³

↓

Ω = 10²³
```

The second state is enormously more probable.

For real systems,

containing

```text
10²³ particles
```

the probability of spontaneously returning to a low-entropy state is practically zero.

Therefore,

entropy appears to

always increase.

# 10. Physical Interpretation

Suppose

a drop of ink

is placed in water.

Initially,

```text
Ink

↓

Small Ω
```

After mixing,

```text
Ink distributed everywhere

↓

Very Large Ω
```

Since

```text
Large Ω

↓

Large Entropy
```

the mixed state is far more probable.

This explains why

ink never

spontaneously separates again.

# 14. Common Misconceptions

❌ Nature "tries" to increase entropy.

✔ Nature does not make decisions.

Systems simply evolve toward the most probable macrostate because it has overwhelmingly more microscopic arrangements.

---

❌ Entropy increases because energy is lost.

✔ Energy is conserved.

Entropy increases because the number of accessible microstates increases.

---

❌ Equilibrium means particles stop moving.

✔ Particles continue to move continuously.

Only the macroscopic properties remain constant.

# 📝 Exam Questions

### Q1. Why is the equilibrium state the most probable state?

Because the equilibrium macrostate corresponds to the largest number of microstates (maximum multiplicity), making it statistically the most likely state.

---

### Q2. Explain the relationship between multiplicity and entropy.

Entropy is related to multiplicity by Boltzmann's equation

```text
S = k ln Ω
```

Hence, larger multiplicity corresponds to larger entropy.

---

### Q3. Give the statistical interpretation of the Second Law.

The Second Law states that spontaneous processes occur in the direction of increasing multiplicity. Since entropy is proportional to the logarithm of multiplicity, spontaneous processes also occur in the direction of increasing entropy.

---

### Q4. Why does entropy become maximum at equilibrium?

At equilibrium, the system occupies the macrostate with the largest number of possible microscopic arrangements. Therefore, multiplicity is maximum, and according to Boltzmann's equation, entropy is also maximum.

---

# ⭐ Final Conclusion

Statistical Thermodynamics explains the **Second Law of Thermodynamics** using probability.

Nature evolves toward the macrostate with the largest number of microscopic arrangements because this state is overwhelmingly more probable.

Since

```text
S = k ln Ω
```

the most probable state is also the state of **maximum entropy**, which is the **equilibrium state**.
